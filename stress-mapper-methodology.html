Stress Mapper Methodology

Technical note on construct design, scoring logic, weighting rationale, and limitations for the GlassCase Institutional Stress Mapper.

1. Purpose and scope

The Institutional Stress Mapper is a structured triage tool for detecting patterns of institutional stress over time. It supports comparative pattern detection and prioritisation. It does not establish factual findings, legal conclusions, or diagnoses.

The tool is designed for use by oversight practitioners, researchers, educators, and anyone seeking to structure their interpretation of observable institutional signals. It sits in the tradition of risk-based regulatory triage — identifying where deeper inquiry may be warranted, not determining what happened or why.

What this tool is not. It is not an audit. It is not an assessment. It does not claim measurement precision. Scores represent user judgement based on observable proxy indicators, not validated findings. Output is not suitable for use as evidence in legal, regulatory, or administrative proceedings without independent professional verification.

2. Stress, not trauma

The tool deliberately measures stress, not trauma. This distinction matters. Stress is a load condition — observable, measurable, and present in all organisations at varying levels. Trauma is a downstream consequence that occurs when stress exceeds institutional capacity over time. The literature supports this framing: Vivian and Hormann's work on organisational trauma (2013) developed a phenomenological framework for understanding how trauma enters and moves through organisations, identifying distinct entry points for trauma and entry points for healing. Their model treats trauma as the object of study — the outcome. This tool treats stress as the measured variable and references trauma only as a potential downstream consequence.

This is not a semantic choice. It is a posture choice. Measuring "stress" positions the tool as diagnostic and forward-looking. Measuring "trauma" would position it as retrospective and potentially accusatory. The tool identifies where load is accumulating; it does not diagnose what that load has done to the organisation or its people.

3. Constructs and subsystems

The tool decomposes institutional health into five subsystems. These are not exhaustive; they represent the most commonly observable and externally verifiable dimensions of institutional stress in public-sector and education contexts.

Subsystem Observable proxies Why included
Leadership & Governance Decision quality, turnover, structural changes, acting appointments Leadership instability is a leading indicator of systemic stress across all other dimensions
Workforce Stability Staff churn, vacancies, secondments, LOA spikes Workforce disruption both signals and amplifies institutional strain
Transparency & Compliance FOI volume, refusal rates, processing times, deemed refusals FOI and compliance data are the most publicly verifiable signals of governance health
Organisational Climate Morale indicators, grievances, cultural signals, survey data Climate captures the human experience of institutional stress that quantitative metrics miss
External Scrutiny Audits, OVIC, WorkSafe, Ombudsman, media External engagement reflects both the severity and visibility of institutional problems

These five subsystems are informed by organisational failure literature (Reason, Vaughan, Perrow), institutional crisis theory (Boin & 't Hart), safety culture frameworks (Westrum), regulatory craft (Sparrow), and responsive regulation theory (Braithwaite). They are not derived from a single model but represent a synthesis of dimensions that recur across these traditions.

The subsystem decomposition reflects Reason's model of latent failures accumulating across organisational layers before surfacing as active failures. The temporal dimension — scoring across multiple time periods — draws on Perrow's thesis that in complex, tightly coupled systems, cascading failures are inherent properties of the system rather than aberrations. Vaughan's concept of normalisation of deviance explains why the timeline view matters: each tolerated deviation resets the institutional baseline, so trigger incidents compound rather than resolve.

The Transparency & Compliance subsystem is weighted highest (×1.3) in part because FOI data provides the strongest publicly verifiable signal. OAIC quarterly statistics show FOI refusal rates as a measurable, longitudinal indicator of governance health. Surges in FOI volume and rising refusal rates have been documented as stress signals on institutions themselves, not just on applicants.

4. Scoring scale

Each subsystem is scored on a 0–3 ordinal scale per time period:

Score Band Meaning
0 Baseline No observable stress signals beyond normal operational variation
1 Elevated Early or isolated signals that may warrant monitoring
2 Acute Clear stress signals across multiple indicators; active concern
3 Chronic Sustained, cross-subsystem stress with no recovery trajectory

This is an ordinal scale, not interval or ratio. A score of 2 does not mean "twice as stressed" as 1. The bands represent qualitative states, not quantities. The scale is deliberately coarse — designed for triage-level pattern detection, not granular measurement.

At Acute (2) and Chronic (3) levels, the tool prompts the user to record a justification note explaining what observable signal supports the rating. This is a methodological guardrail: high scores without documented reasoning are analytically weak and should be treated with caution.

5. Composite index and weighting

Individual subsystem scores are combined into a single Stress Index (0–10 scale) using a weighted arithmetic mean, normalised to the 0–10 range.

Why weights exist

Equal weighting is not neutral — it is an implicit value judgement that all subsystems contribute equally to institutional stress. Differential weighting allows the tool to reflect that some signals are more externally verifiable, more reliably observable, or more strongly predictive of systemic problems.

Current weights

Subsystem Weight Rationale
Leadership & Governance ×1.2 Leadership instability is a strong leading indicator with downstream effects across all subsystems
Workforce Stability ×1.0 Standard baseline weight
Transparency & Compliance ×1.3 FOI and compliance data are the most publicly verifiable signals; repeated statutory non-compliance is a strong proxy for systemic governance stress
Organisational Climate ×1.0 Standard baseline weight
External Scrutiny ×1.1 External oversight engagement reflects severity and public visibility of institutional problems
Sensitivity. These weights are provisional expert estimates reflecting relative signal observability and reliability. We tested whether reasonable alternative weights (including equal weighting) materially change trend shape; they generally do not, which suggests observed patterns are not weight-driven. The weighting range is deliberately narrow (1.0–1.3) to prevent any single subsystem from dominating the composite.

Severity bands

The 0–10 Stress Index is mapped to four severity bands:

  • Baseline (0–2.5) — Normal operational range
  • Elevated (2.5–5) — Detectable strain; monitoring warranted
  • Acute (5–7.5) — Active stress; intervention may be warranted
  • Chronic (7.5–10) — Sustained systemic stress

These bands are evenly distributed across the 0–10 scale. They represent communicative scaffolding for legibility, not empirically validated cutpoints. The graduated escalation logic draws on Ayres and Braithwaite's responsive regulation framework (1992) — the enforcement pyramid turned sideways into a diagnostic scale. Each band implies a different posture: monitoring, active attention, intervention consideration, and sustained systemic concern.

6. Data handling

All data entered into the Stress Mapper is stored locally in the user's browser (localStorage). No data is transmitted to GlassCase servers. Data persists across browser sessions on the same device until the user clears it or resets the tool.

Users can export their data as a structured JSON file. The export includes all scores, justification notes, computed indices, and full metadata (tool version, methodology reference, disclaimer). Justification notes are user commentary — they are included in exports but are not published or shared unless the user chooses to distribute the file.

7. Limitations and non-claims

  • This tool does not measure institutional health. It structures user interpretation of observable signals.
  • Scores are ordinal, not interval. Arithmetic operations on ordinal data (including the weighted mean) are a pragmatic approximation, not a statistical claim.
  • The five subsystems are not exhaustive. They represent commonly observable dimensions, not a complete model of institutional function.
  • Weights are provisional. They reflect design judgement informed by the literature, not empirical calibration against outcome data.
  • The tool does not establish causation, intent, motive, or wrongdoing.
  • Output is not suitable for use as evidence in legal, regulatory, or administrative proceedings without independent professional verification.
  • Narrative timelines (demonstration only) carry legal, ethical, and reputational risk. The exemplar included in the tool is entirely fictional.

OECD. OECD Public Integrity Indicators. OECD Publishing, 2024. — Dimensional precedent for decomposing institutional integrity into observable subsystems.

Sparrow, M.K. The Regulatory Craft: Controlling Risks, Solving Problems, and Managing Compliance. Brookings Institution Press, 2000. — Risk-based triage logic and the distinction between pattern detection and determination.

Reason, J. Managing the Risks of Organizational Accidents. Ashgate, 1997. — Organisational accident theory and the concept of latent conditions producing systemic failure.

Vaughan, D. The Challenger Launch Decision: Risky Technology, Culture, and Deviance at NASA. University of Chicago Press, 1996. — Normalisation of deviance and how institutional signals are missed or reinterpreted.

Westrum, R. "A Typology of Organisational Cultures." BMJ Quality & Safety, 13(suppl 2), ii22–ii27, 2004. — Organisational culture typology (pathological, bureaucratic, generative) as a framework for interpreting climate signals.

Braithwaite, J. Restorative Justice and Responsive Regulation. Oxford University Press, 2002. — Responsive regulation theory and the escalation logic that informs severity band design.

Perrow, C. Normal Accidents: Living with High-Risk Technologies. Basic Books, 1984. — Cascading failure in complex, tightly coupled systems as an inherent property rather than an aberration. Theoretical basis for the timeline heat bar and escalation patterns.

Boin, A. & 't Hart, P. "Institutional crises in policy sectors." In Wagenaar, H. (ed.) Government Institutions: Effects, Changes and Normative Foundations. Kluwer, 2000. See also: Boin, A., 't Hart, P., Stern, E. & Sundelius, B. The Politics of Crisis Management: Public Leadership Under Pressure. Cambridge University Press, 2005. — Institutional crisis as a systemic concept distinct from individual incidents.

Ayres, I. & Braithwaite, J. Responsive Regulation: Transcending the Deregulation Debate. Oxford University Press, 1992. — The enforcement pyramid and graduated regulatory response based on signals. Precedent for the severity band escalation logic.

Vivian, P. & Hormann, S. Organizational Trauma and Healing. CreateSpace, 2013. See also: Hormann, S. "The Strengths and Shadows Model." Humanistic Management Journal, 3(1), 91–104, 2018. — Foundational work on organisational trauma as a systemic concept. Informs the stress-vs-trauma framing distinction: stress is the load condition; trauma is the outcome when stress exceeds capacity over time.

OAIC. FOI Statistics. Published quarterly. oaic.gov.au. — Raw data source for FOI volume, refusal rates, and processing times as longitudinal institutional health indicators.
