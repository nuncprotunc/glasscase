Edition 2: Reasons for Decisions

February 5, 2026 · Jay Spudvilas

The Problem

When an agency refuses an FOI request under section 47E(d) of the Freedom of Information Act 1982, what does the law actually require them to explain?

Section 47E(d) allows an agency to refuse access to documents if disclosure would have a substantial adverse effect on the proper and efficient conduct of the agency's operations.

The test has three parts:

  • Which documents would cause the harm?
  • What is the nature of the harm?
  • Why would disclosure cause a substantial adverse effect?

In 'YF' and Services Australia [2021] AICmr 52, the agency's reasons failed all three.

The YF Decision Anatomy

What the Agency Provided

The agency's reasons were generic and conclusory:

  • s 47E(d) claimed → "Document-specific analysis"
  • "Disclosure would allow members of the public to circumvent established channels" → Which documents? Which staff details? What evidence of harm?
  • Generic assertion → Particular adverse effect
  • "In all circumstances" → For these documents
  • Class claim → Case-by-case weighing

× Agency fails to discharge s 55D onus

What the Law Required

The Information Commissioner found the agency failed to meet its burden under section 55D because it did not explain:

  • Which specific documents contained the sensitive information
  • What particular harm would result from disclosure
  • Why the harm would be substantial rather than minor or theoretical

The Commissioner noted that class claims—refusing entire categories of documents without individual assessment—are inconsistent with the FOI Act's document-by-document approach.

The Pattern

This isn't unique to Services Australia. The same gap appears across agencies:

  • Agencies assert harm without evidence
  • Reasons are copied from guidelines rather than tailored to the request
  • The onus under section 55D is treated as satisfied by assertion alone

The law requires reasons that allow review. Generic statements don't meet that standard.
